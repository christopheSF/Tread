High PriorityRequired vs Optional fields — the #1 onboarding question
In Igel session #8, Dalton asked "is drop-off required?", "is material required?", and "do I need a vendor rate?" in the same 40-minute call. The answer is always "not required but recommended for reporting" — but this is never documented. Customers are guessing or asking their CSM on every call.
📍 George Igel Onboarding #8 (May 28) — multiple explicit questions
Suggestion: Add a "Required vs Recommended" table to the Create Project/Order page. Two columns: what happens if you skip it, and what reporting you lose. One table replaces a recurring 5-minute CSM explanation.
High Priority"Running" jobs — on-site hauling with no fixed load target
The Running checkbox came up in both Igel sessions. Tim recommended setting it at the project level so dispatchers never forget. No documentation explains what Running mode does differently: geofence triggers still fire, loads still accumulate, but the job won't auto-end after hitting the truck load target. Critical for on-site earthmoving.
📍 George Igel #7 (May 27) and #8 (May 28)
Suggestion: Add a "Running vs Fixed Load" explainer to the Orders & Projects core concept page. Include: what it means, when to use it, and Tim's recommendation to set it at the project level. A single callout box covers 80% of onboarding confusion on this topic.
High PriorityProject → Order → Job → Ticket cascade — missing from the landing page
The video's central message — "project is the template, order is the daily instance, job is the driver assignment" — was also restated verbally by Tim in both Igel sessions. Customers who read the docs before onboarding don't understand the cascade, and CSMs repeat this explanation on every kickoff call.
📍 Loom video (core theme) + George Igel #7 (Tim restated 3×)
Suggestion: Add a Project → Order → Job → Ticket → Approval flow diagram as the first element on docs.tread.ai. Even a text-based version removes the need for CSMs to explain this verbally on every kickoff.
New from CallsChild Account vs Internal Trucks — the fleet architecture decision
George Igel spent 20+ minutes debating whether their subsidiary (DBM dump trucks) should be a child Tread account or internal drivers. The trade-off: child account = better for future separate dispatcher, but back-office approval requires toggling accounts. Internal = simpler dispatch but everyone sees the fleet. This decision must be made before onboarding begins — it affects the entire account structure.
📍 George Igel #7 (May 27) — extensive discussion
Suggestion: Add a "Fleet Architecture Decision" guide to the Onboarding section. Decision tree: Are these trucks a different legal entity? Need a separate dispatcher? Customers outside your account? → Internal vs Child vs Vendor.
New from CallsVendor onboarding playbook and communication templates
Reynaga had one week to onboard 10 vendor companies and 30+ drivers before a June 8 go-live. Latefa had to manually draft a vendor communication template. This doesn't exist as a self-serve resource — every CSM creates it ad hoc. For brokers, mass vendor onboarding is the hardest part of launch and the most time-sensitive.
📍 Reynaga Transportation onboarding (May 29)
Suggestion: Create a "Vendor Onboarding Playbook" page with: (1) sample text/email template to send vendors, (2) what vendors must do before first dispatch, (3) bulk import vs. individual invite. Include the Tread support number in the template. Removes a CSM-dependent step entirely.
New from CallsHow to test the driver and foreman experience yourself
Dalton Zemba asked directly: "Can I create a separate account with my personal email to test as a foreman?" Tim answered yes — but noted company admins see extra tabs in the mobile app that foremen don't. This is an important nuance for any dispatcher-admin doing UAT. Not documented anywhere.
📍 George Igel #8 (May 28)

Medium PriorityJob state machine — Pending → In Review → Completed not documented
The video ends with the driver clicking Submit and the job going to Completed — but the Completed → Approved transition (where billing feeds from) is skipped. Foreman approval flows exist in Specialized Workflows, but the job status model isn't explained as a concept anywhere.

Tread Approvals & Billing — Completing the Loop

Companion script to "Projects, Orders & Jobs Explained." Covers the Approvals page, ticket review, shift approval, and QuickBooks export — the lifecycle step missing from all current onboarding videos. Estimated runtime: ~4 min.

Gap AddressedJob state machine not documented past Completed
This script directly addresses Gap #7 from the analysis above: the Completed → Approved → Billed transition is skipped in all existing videos and docs. Every CSM explains this verbally on every kickoff call. Recording this as a standalone video removes that dependency.
00:00 – 01:15
Chapter 1 — Where We Left Off: The Job Lifecycle
0:00In the previous video, we covered how to create a project, build an order, and send jobs to your drivers. In this video, we're going to pick up right where that left off — and walk through what happens after a driver completes a job.
0:16When a driver finishes their loads for the day, they tap Stop Job and confirm. Their status moves from Active to In Review.
0:27In Review means the driver can now look back at every ticket they uploaded throughout the day — the scale ticket photo, the ticket number that was automatically read from the image, and the quantity.
0:42If they missed a ticket on the road, they can still add it here — before submitting. This is the driver's final check to make sure every load is captured.
0:55When the driver is satisfied, they tap Submit. The job status moves to Completed. From your dispatcher view, you'll see that job card update in real time.
1:05But Completed doesn't mean done for billing. It means the driver's work is captured and ready for your review. That's where the Approvals page comes in.
01:16 – 02:50
Chapter 2 — The Approvals Page
1:16Head over to the Approvals tab in your top navigation. This is where all completed driver shifts come to live — your daily billing queue.
1:28Each row here represents one driver's shift. You can see their name, shift start and end time, total hours, number of loads, and ticket count at a glance.
1:42Let's click into a shift. You'll see every load that driver ran — the ticket photo, the ticket number, material quantity, and the pickup and dropoff site for each trip.
1:58Tread automatically flags anomalies. If a ticket number was already recorded on another load, it shows a duplicate warning. If a load is missing a ticket entirely, that's flagged too. You don't have to hunt for problems — the system surfaces them.
2:15This is your quality check before approving. You can open each ticket image to verify the quantities, and make any corrections before locking this shift in.
2:28For drivers who are still running loads, you'll see an End Shift button. This lets you manually close their shift from the dispatcher side — useful at end of day if a driver forgets to submit on their own.
2:40You can also select multiple rows and bulk-approve shifts when everything looks clean. That's a big time saver on high-volume days.
02:51 – 04:05
Chapter 3 — Approving, Exporting, and Billing
2:51When you're ready, click Approve Shift. That shift is now locked. The load data, ticket numbers, hours, and quantities are finalized — nothing can change after approval.
3:05Approval is what feeds your billing. Tread uses the approved data to calculate what your customer owes, based on the rate you set at the project or order level — per ton, per load, or per hour.
3:18To generate your invoice export, head to the Reports section. Here you'll find the QuickBooks Export — a CSV formatted for direct import into QuickBooks Online or Desktop.
3:30The export pulls from approved shifts only. Anything that hasn't been approved yet won't appear. So before running your weekly export, make sure all shifts for the billing period have been approved.
3:44You can filter by date range, by customer, or by project. Once you've set your filters, download the CSV and import it directly into QuickBooks — your line items, quantities, and rates will all be there.
3:57And that's the full loop. Project → Order → Job → Ticket → Approval → Invoice. Once your first week is done, this becomes your daily rhythm — drivers submit, you approve, and billing runs itself.
